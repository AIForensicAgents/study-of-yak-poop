# study-of-yak-poop

A field guide and open study repository for yak scat (droppings, feces) — designed for educators, field biologists, citizen scientists, and anyone curious about what yak poop can tell us about animals, ecosystems, and seasons.

---

## Overview

This repository collects notes, observations, photos, and guidance for the non-invasive study of yak feces in the field. It is intended to be educational, rigorous, and — yes — a little bit fun. Content focuses on observational morphology, habitat and diet inference, safety/ethics, and ways to contribute responsibly to biological knowledge.

Use this README as a high-level primer before diving into field work, sample logging, or image curation. For lab protocols and genetic assays, consult institutional biosafety procedures and domain-specific literature.

---

## Why it matters

- **Ecosystem monitoring:** Yak droppings are informative indicators of population density, movement patterns, and seasonal range use.
- **Dietary inference:** Contents such as plant fragments, seeds, and insect remains reveal foraging behavior and habitat preferences.
- **Nutrient cycling:** Scat contributes to soil fertility and supports microhabitats for invertebrates and fungi.
- **Non-invasive study:** Examining feces is a low-impact way to gather biological data without handling or stressing animals.

Studying scat supports conservation decisions, pasture management, and educational outreach — all while minimizing disturbance to wildlife.

## Morphology notes

When documenting samples, record the following morphological features consistently:

- **Form & Size:** Pelletized, tubular, flattened, or clumped; approximate length/diameter or pellet count.
- **Texture:** Firm, crumbly, gelatinous, dry, tacky — an indicator of hydration and diet.
- **Color:** Fresh vs. aged colors (fresh often darker and shinier; older becomes gray/brittle from sun/oxidation).
- **Contents:** Visible plant fibers, grass blades, leaf fragments, seeds, insect parts, hair/fur.
- **Odor:** Noted qualitatively (mild, pungent, ammonia-ish) — useful in assessing freshness but subjective and time-sensitive.
- **Surface & inclusions:** Presence of mold, larval tunnels, pollen clumps, or parasitic egg clusters (visual only — do not handle).
- **Spatial context:** Single pile vs. latrine site (repeated deposits often mark trails or territorial points).

Standardize photos: include a scale (ruler or a coin), overhead and angled shots, and notes on sunlight and humidity to support later comparative analysis.

## Habitat / Diet clues

Interpreting what yak scat tells us about diet and habitat:

- **High fiber, coarse plant fragments:** Grazing on coarse grasses or sedges; likely alpine pasture feeding.
- **Abundant seeds or fruit remains:** Suggests foraging in berry-bearing shrubs or valley habitats.
- **Fine grass cuticle and little fibrous material:** May indicate grazing on new shoots or closely cropped pasture.
- **High ash content or mineral granules:** May reflect geophagy (soil ingestion) common in mineral-rich foraging areas.
- **Insect fragments or chitin:** Supplementary feeding behavior or grazing in insect-rich microhabitats.
- **Consistency shifts by season:** Wetter, softer scat often in rainy months; drier, pelletized in cold/dry periods.

Combine scat observations with vegetation surveys, GPS location, elevation, and seasonal notes for robust habitat inference.

## Ethics / Safety

Safety and ethics are paramount. Please adhere to the following:

- **Do not disturb animals:** Observe from a distance. Avoid altering or moving fresh deposits in ways that would change animal behavior.
- **Permits & permissions:** Obtain required research permits and landowner permissions before sampling or extensive site work.
- **Personal protective equipment (PPE):** Use disposable gloves, eye protection, and masks as appropriate. Avoid touching face and exposed skin.
- **Sample handling:** If collecting samples for lab analysis, use leak-proof, labeled containers. Follow cold-chain and biosafety protocols.
- **Decontamination:** Clean tools and boots after fieldwork to prevent cross-site contamination and pathogen spread.
- **Respect cultural contexts:** In many regions, livestock and wildlife have cultural significance. Engage local communities and follow local practices.
- **Non-diagnostic on sight:** Never assume a health or parasitic status without laboratory confirmation; visual signs are preliminary only.

When in doubt, prioritize safety, permissions, and the well-being of animals and local people.

## Future work

Planned and suggested directions for the project:

- Build a standardized data schema (JSON/CSV) for scat observations: location, GPS, elevation, date/time, morphology fields, photo links, observer metadata.
- Citizen science mobile app for geo-tagged submissions and automated triage.
- Image classification using machine learning to cluster morphology types and estimate freshness.
- Collaborative studies: gut microbiome sequencing, plant metabarcoding from feces, parasite surveys (with appropriate permits and biosafety).
- Spatial analysis: map latrine sites, movement corridors, and seasonal range overlap with pasture management zones.
- Educational modules and outreach kits for schools and field courses.

Contributions, protocols, and reproducible analysis pipelines are welcome via pull requests and issues. Please follow the CONTRIBUTING.md and local ethical guidelines.
